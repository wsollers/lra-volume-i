\small
Lambda calculus ($\lambda$-calculus) is a formal system for expressing
computation through function abstraction and application.
Introduced by Alonzo Church (1936) to formalise computability,
it is the theoretical foundation of functional programming
and is equivalent in power to Turing machines.

\medskip
\textbf{Why it belongs here:} Lambda calculus sits at the intersection
of logic, set theory, and computation.
The Curry--Howard correspondence connects it directly to the
predicate logic and type theory in this volume:
propositions are types, proofs are programs, and
implication corresponds to function type.

\medskip
\textbf{Primary source:} Barendregt \& Barendsen,
\textit{Introduction to Lambda Calculus} (free PDF).
Secondary: Rojas, \textit{A Tutorial Introduction to the Lambda Calculus}.
Bridge to types: Pierce, \textit{Types and Programming Languages} Ch.\ 5, 8, 9.
\end{tcolorbox}

\small
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Syntax} --- terms, variables, abstraction $\lambda x.M$,
        application $MN$, free and bound variables
  \item \textbf{Reduction} --- $\alpha$-conversion, $\beta$-reduction,
        $\eta$-reduction, normal forms
  \item \textbf{Church Encodings} --- booleans, natural numbers (Church numerals),
        pairs, lists
  \item \textbf{Fixed Points} --- the Y-combinator, recursive functions
  \item \textbf{Computability} --- $\lambda$-definability, equivalence with
        Turing machines, undecidability of $\beta$-equality
  \item \textbf{Typed Lambda Calculus} --- simple types, type inference,
        Curry--Howard correspondence
\end{enumerate}
\end{tcolorbox}
